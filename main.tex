\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The YOLO (You Only Look Once) series of object detection models have always been at the forefront of the field of computer vision due to their speed and efficiency. The latest version, YOLOv8, introduces several major advances aimed at improving detection accuracy. This report motivates the use of YOLOv8 as the backbone of a dedicated small object detection network and summarizes the improvements compared to previous generations.

\medskip
\noindent
YOLOv8’s core enhancements revolve around architectural modifications designed to address the challenges associated with detecting small objects. To address this challenge, our improvement introduces a shuffle attention module with an attention mechanism. This mechanism is specifically designed to enhance the sensitivity of features from different channels of the model while barely affecting processing speed. We also used the YOLOv8-P2 structure, in which the added detection head for small object detection improved the final accuracy.

\medskip
\noindent
Comparative testing of our newly proposed YOLOv8 model with previous baselines shows significant improvements, with a 12\% improvement in mAP50

\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

\medskip
In the context of current technological advancements, drone aerial photography technology has been widely applied in multiple fields such as agricultural monitoring, geographical mapping, urban planning, and more. With the continuous growth in application demands, there is an increasing need for precision and efficiency in detecting small objects in drone aerial photography. The demand for real-time image processing and object detection on drone platforms is growing day by day. 

\medskip
\textbf{This technology has a wide range of applications across key areas including:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Traffic monitoring:} By detecting and identifying vehicles and pedestrians in real time, object detection technology can significantly improve the efficiency of managing traffic flows.
    \item \textbf{Agricultural pest control:} Used in precision agriculture, the object detection system carried by drones can effectively monitor and identify pests on crops.
    \item \textbf{Wildlife protection:} In protected areas, object detection technology can help researchers monitor the behavior and abundance of wild animals to develop more effective conservation measures.
\end{itemize}


\medskip
Traditional object detection models often face challenges of low accuracy and slow processing speeds when dealing with small objects in high-altitude photography. To address these challenges, this report proposes an improved method based on the YOLOv8 model, aimed at optimizing the model structure and introducing attention mechanisms, making it more suitable for detecting small objects in high-altitude drone aerial photography environments.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{img000010.jpg}}
\caption{Sample image from the UADTV dataset \cite{uavdt}}
\label {Fig1}
\end{figure}




Therefore, this study proposes several targeted improvements:
\begin{itemize}
    \item By adjusting the parameter configuration of YOLOv8, further improve the algorithm's recognition accuracy for specific small objects;
    \item Utilizing the anchor-free feature of YOLOv8 to simplify the algorithm structure, aiming to increase operational speed for smooth operation on smaller devices;
    \item Test and integrate the YOLOv8-P2 structure, adding a detection head for small objects
    \item Combining YOLOv8's FPN network and introducing the attention mechanism from Transformers to achieve comprehensive utilization of high-level and low-level semantics in images, thereby achieving more accurate identification of small objects in the high-altitude, unobstructed environments.\cite{9607536}
    
\end{itemize}

\medskip
To achieve these improvement objectives, this study selected the annotated VisDrone dataset as the training and validation set. The VisDrone dataset contains a wealth of small objects from a high-altitude perspective, making it an ideal choice for evaluating the performance of drone object detection algorithms. 

\medskip
Finally, to test the effectiveness of the proposed improvements through training and validation on VisDrone, the UAVDT dataset was used as the test set to assess performance. The UAVDT dataset includes a large number of drone-captured videos, particularly emphasizing the problem of dynamic small object detection in complex environments. Its diverse scenarios, different weather conditions, and time periods provide a good platform for testing the robustness and applicability of the algorithm.

\section{Related Works}

\subsection{Object detection}
The task of object detection is to identify all objects of interest in an image and determine their categories and locations. It is one of the core issues in the field of computer vision. Object detection has always been one of the most challenging problems in the field of computer vision due to the different appearances, shapes, and postures of different objects, coupled with factors such as lighting and obstacles during the imaging process.

\medskip
There are four major categories of image recognition tasks in computer vision:\\
(1) Classification: Given a picture or a video, determine what category of objects it contains.\\
(2) Positioning-Location: Locate the location of this object.\\
(3) Detection: Locate the location of the object and know what the object is.\\
(4) Segmentation: It is divided into instance segmentation (Instance-level) and scene segmentation (Scene-level) to solve the problem of "which object or scene each pixel belongs to."

\medskip
Our task is the third type of object detection, detecting the location and category of the object in the picture
\subsubsection{One-stage object detection}
Single-stage object detection has gained widespread application in recent years due to its real-time processing capabilities. For example, YOLO (You Only Look Once)\cite{redmon2016look} and SSD (Single Shot MultiBox Detector)\cite{Liu_2016} predict the category and location of the object directly from the image through a separate neural network model, omitting the candidate area proposal step in the traditional two-stage method. The main advantage is their efficiency, they enable faster inference without sacrificing too much accuracy, which makes them ideal for applications that require real-time processing. Among them, the YOLO series achieves fast and efficient object detection by dividing the image into grids and predicting multiple bounding boxes and category probabilities simultaneously on each grid unit.

\subsubsection{Two-stage object detection}
Two-stage object detection technology, such as R-CNN\cite{girshick2014rich} and its variants (Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN\cite{ren2016faster}), first uses a Region Proposal Network (RPN) in the first stage to generate potential object candidate regions. In the second stage, these candidate areas are then sent to a deep convolutional network for feature extraction, and the category and precise location of the object are finally determined. 

\medskip
The advantage of dual-stage object detection is that through two-stage processing, objects in the image can be more accurately located and classified. However, the disadvantages are also obvious. When generating a large number of regional suggestions in the first stage, computing resources are consumed greatly, which may not be efficient enough in real-time applications.

\subsubsection{YOLOv8}
Originally trained on the COCO dataset, YOLOv8 excels at identifying large objects with high accuracy.

\subsubsection{Anchor-free methods}
Anchor-free technology is an Object detection method that does not use predefined anchors. Traditional anchor-based detectors, such as Faster R-CNN and SSD, rely on preset anchor frames with different sizes, scales, and aspect ratios to predict the location of objects in images. These anchor points serve as candidate boxes, which are adjusted and classified through the network to determine the final object boundaries and categories. 

\medskip
However, it also brings some challenges, such as the complexity of anchor point selection and the tuning of a large number of hyperparameters. Anchor-free technology is also used in YOLOv8, which is what we will improve.

\subsection{Transformer(ViT)}
Vision Transformer (ViT)\cite{dosovitskiy2021image} is a groundbreaking technology in the field of computer vision. It adopts the Transformer architecture and was originally mainly used for natural language processing. The core innovation is to decompose images into small 16x16 image patches, treating them as elements in a sequence, similar to words in text processing. These image patches are then converted to embedding vectors throught linear projection and fed into a standard Transformer encoder. 

\medskip
Furthermore, ViT introduces position embedding to preserve the position information of image patches. ViT is also widely used in Object Detection tasks. Figure 3 shows the structure of ViT

\subsubsection{Shuffle Attention}
Shuffle attention\cite{yang2021sanet} is a variation of vit, which uses Shuffle Units to effectively combine two types of attention mechanisms.Specifically, SA first groups channel dimensions into multiple sub-features before processing them in parallel. Then, for each sub-feature, SA utilizes a Shuffle Unit to depict feature dependencies in both spatial and channel dimensions. The core advantage of this mechanism lies in its lightweight and high efficiency, because it allows the network to maintain low computational costs while enhancing feature processing capabilities by limiting the increase in parameters and computational complexity.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Dataset Selection}
We chose to use VisDrone-2019 as the training test and validation set of the model because this data set has the following advantages: First, the data set covers a wide range of terrains, weather conditions, and lighting situations. recognition ability in various environments. Secondly, VisDrone contains more than 2.6 million manually annotated bounding boxes, covering a variety of objects, providing rich annotation information to help the model learn and understand small objects in complex scenes. In addition, the data set contains 288 video clips and 10,209 still images, totaling 261,908 frames of images, providing large-scale data resources for model training. 

\medskip
It is worth mentioning that VisDrone is a pre-split data set, which means that the data has been organized into training and testing subsets, making it easy to use directly and accelerating the model development and verification process. These features make VisDrone an ideal choice for small object detection research and application development.\cite{zhu2021detection}

We also used UAVDT\cite{u}, an open source unlabeled UAV high-altitude aerial photography data set, for universal testing.

\subsection{YOLOv8-P2 structure}
The P2 structure of YOLOv8 is a configuration publicly released by the YOLOv8 team, specifically enhanced for detecting small objects.
It includes an additional detection head for small objects compared to the basic structure, mainly collecting information from the P3 layer, which corresponds to the early stages of the image entering the network.

\subsection{Shuffle Attention Module}
Due to the characteristics of the P2 structure mentioned above, the first c2f layer collects information about small objects on a large scale in the early stage of neural network, so I decided to use attention mechanisms to enhance this part. After the first c2f layer in YOLOv8, a shuffle attention module is added to mix channel features from different layers, as shown in Figure 5. This module utilizes the shuffle attention's ability to mix multi-channel information, which significantly enhances the model's accuracy.

\subsection{Experimental base on TOOD}

YOLOv8 leverages the latest advancements in anchor-free technology, introducing a novel method for enhancing detection performance by aligning detection tasks with specific object characteristics, as proposed by ``TOOD: Task-aligned One-stage Object Detection.''\cite{feng2021tood} 

\medskip
This approach posits that synchronizing detection tasks with the inherent features of objects can lead to higher precision, particularly in the demanding scenario of small object detection from aerial imagery. Building upon the TOOD framework, this study examines the use of the RotatedTaskAlignedAssigner within the YOLOv8 architecture, aiming to improve model accuracy for small object detection by adjusting the quantity of prediction boxes. This adjustment is hypothesized to optimize the balance between detection sensitivity and specificity, thus enhancing overall performance for detecting small-scale objects.

\subsection{Hypothesis}

The hypothesis of this research is based on the characteristic of small objects occupying minimal area in images, asserting that a smaller number of appropriately-sized proposal boxes can sufficiently envelop these objects. Such coverage is theorized not only to quicken the processing speed but also to amplify detection precision. By calibrating the quantity and dimensions of these prediction boxes through the RotatedTaskAlignedAssigner, the model is expected to yield superior results.

\subsection{Technical Detail in Task-aligned Loss Function from TOOD}
A pivotal element of the ``TOOD: Task-aligned One-stage Object Detection'' framework is its Task-aligned loss function, epitomized by the equation \( t = s^\alpha \times u^\beta \), where \( t \) represents the task-aligned training target, a product of the classification confidence score \( s \) and the predicted utility score \( u \).

\medskip
Within this framework, \( t \) is calculated for multiple positive sample points holding the maximum \( t \) values, while the rest are deemed negative. These positive sample points are most likely to match actual objects within the image, and the objective is to maximize their prediction accuracy.

\medskip
A lower \textit{topk} implies that the model focuses on a smaller subset of high-confidence predictions, which is particularly pertinent for the small objects present in the VisDrone dataset, often appearing as minuscule regions within aerial shots. Diminishing the \textit{topk} value allows the model to give precedence to the most likely regions for object occurrence, potentially leading to more accurate and expedited detection.



\section{Experimental Result}

\subsection{Experiment Setup}
All experiments were conducted on the Ubuntu 22.04 operating system, utilizing a single NVIDIA Tesla T4 graphics card for training.

\subsubsection*{Hyperparameter Settings}

In our experiments, we configured the following key hyperparameters to optimize the training process of the model:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Image Size (\texttt{imgsz})}: The input image size used for model training was set to 640 pixels.
    \item \textbf{Training Epochs (\texttt{epochs})}: The model was iterated over the training data for 150 epochs.Except baseline model which setting is 100 epochs. The reason is the baseline model has converged after 80 epoch.
    \item \textbf{Intersection over Union Threshold (\texttt{iou})}: Set at 0.6, this parameter is used for object detection criteria during training.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{close\_mosaic} Parameter}: Set to 10, this parameter regulates the density of image mosaicking during data augmentation, enhancing the model's adaptability to complex scenes.
    \item \textbf{Optimizer (\texttt{optimizer})}: Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) was chosen as the optimization algorithm to  control the convergence speed and stability.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}



In image compression, the techniques can be categorized as: a) Hand-crafted and b) Learning-Based. The traditional, standardized, image compression standards like JPEG \cite{Leger1991} and BPG \cite{Albalawi2015} uses a human-planned pipeline that accepts an input image, apply some transformations, until producing a compressed version of the image that keeps the same information in the image but with some distortion. Therefore, they are called lossy compression. In lossless compression, the algorithms find patterns in the data that could be replaced by a smaller character sequence to save space. The two primary objectives in lossy compression are \cite{Hu2021}:

\vspace{0.1in}
\begin{itemize}
\setlength\itemsep{1em}
    \item[1.]	Decorrelate the image which helps to encoder smaller pixel values that uses smaller number of bits. 
    \item[2.] 	Discard only the least useful information and keep the important information to maintain the image quality. This is by satisfying the rate-distortion tradeoff in which the pixel bit rate is reduced, to reduce the overall image size, while caring about causing less distortion to keep the image quality as high as possible \cite{DBLP:conf/icml/BlauM19}.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.1in}
For lossy compression, one of the common steps available in the pipeline include transformation like Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) for JPEG or Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) in JPEG 2000 which supports multiresolution image representation to decorrelate images \cite{Leger1991}. Another module is quantization which truncates the least significant information. Some quantization methods include vector quantization and trellis-coded quantization. 

\medskip
In the next step for the lossy algorithms, a coder compresses the decorrelated quantized values with entropy encoding. Examples of such coders include Huffman coding, arithmetic coder \cite{10.1145/214762.214771}, and context-adaptive binary arithmetic coding. The BPG image compression algorithm further reduces the spatial redundancy and improve the quality \cite{Albalawi2015}. 
For the hand-crafted pipelines for image compression, there should be an appropriate design by selecting the modules that works together to produce the best image quality with the least bit rate. It is hard to optimize all the modules together. 

\medskip
With the advance in computing power and machine learning, a new generation of image compression techniques appears in which end-to-end deep learning models for image compression are built. 

\medskip
Autoencoder is one of the commonly used architectures for image compression \cite{Kingma2014}. To build an accurate latent vector for the images, convolutional autoencoder are used in which a combination of dense and convolutional layers is used. In \cite{Kingma2014}, a convolutional autoencoder architecture with differential quantization and entropy estimation for image compression is proposed. This work proposed the use of an energy compaction-based penalty into loss function to achieve better compression results. The architecture is also applied to video compression by using an interpolation loop to the image encoder. 

\medskip
Autoencoder cannot build a distribution that characterizes the data. It is ambiguous what is the range of each element in the latent vector of the autoencoder. Using it as a generative model without an underlying distribution, it is not possible to sample a new instance that is like a certain class of instances. This problem is solved by variational autoencoder which forces the latent vector of each compressed instance to conform to a Gaussian distribution. Based on the mean and standard deviation of the distribution, new instances can be generated that have similar properties to the instances used for generating the distribution.

\medskip
In \cite{Zhou_2019_CVPR_Workshops}, a variational autoencoder is proposed that supports a nonlinear encoder transformation, a soft quantizer, a nonlinear decoder transformation and an entropy estimation module. One of the improvements in this paper is the user of the attention mechanism to direct the model to encode the regions with higher attention with high bitrate. The soft quantizer helps to reduce the loss resulted from the regular quantization process to improve the quality of the reconstructed image. The quantized vector is forwarded to an arithmetic coder to further compress the vector before generating the final bit sequence. Such bit sequence is fed to the arithmetic decoder followed by the decoder of the variational autoencoder to reconstruct the image.

\medskip
A group of researchers at Google created a novel architecture that is based on convolutional and deconvolutional long-short term memory (LSTM) recurrent networks to solve the issues of autoencoders \cite{toderici2016variable}. This work aims to support a variable bit rate to reduce the size of the thumbnails that are displayed in the initial page load process beyond the compression capabilities of the current image compression codecs. This helps the mobile device users, which forms a large fraction of the Internet users, with small
screens and low bandwidth to load the pages of the websites faster. The more bits used in this work, the better the visual quality of the compressed images. 

\medskip
Variational autoencoder is a generative model that tries to encode the input image as a vector which is to be decoded later to reconstruct the output. The model focuses on learning the distribution of the set of images used in training and in some cases the visual quality of the reconstructed images is not high. Using the generative adversarial network (GAN), the output created by the generator is directly compared against the original input using the discriminator \cite{goodfellow2014generative}. This way the model is forced to construct a high-quality output to reduce the loss. GANs can work with high resolution images better than variational autoencoders \cite{mentzer2020highfidelity}.

\medskip
In \cite{Agustsson2019}, a GAN framework is proposed for extreme image compression that works with high-resolution images. For the generator of the GAN, the input image is firstly encoded by an encoder followed by the quantization operation. A latent vector is formed from the quantization output by optionally adding some noise. This latent vector is fed to the generator to generate an image that is consistent with the input image distribution. This mode is called generative compression (GC) The paper also supports a new model called selective generative compression (SC) that filters what to be generated and what to be preserved. A binary map is created to filter what regions to be generated and what to be preserved. The zeros in the map mean the region should be fully generated while regions of ones are preserved.

\medskip
Satisfying only the rate-distortion tradeoff is the main disadvantage this work \cite{Agustsson2019}. One motivation behind using GAN is to generate realistic images with high perception quality. Considering only the rate and distortion does not mean the perception will be high \cite{DBLP:conf/icml/BlauM19}. A low distortion does not mean that a high perceptual quality image will be created. Minimizing the distortion may produce an image that is far away from the distribution of the original image. As a result, the rate-distortion theory should also consider perception as a third factor. This is called the rate-distortion-perception tradeoff. There is a tradeoff between the distortion and perception as reducing the bit rate usually happens by distorting the perception quality \cite{DBLP:conf/icml/BlauM19}.

\medskip
In a recent work \cite{mentzer2020highfidelity}, an image compression framework is built based on GAN that satisfy the rate-distortion-perception theory to obtain visually pleasing reconstructions of the compressed images while reducing the bit rate. It supports 3 different bit rates: low, medium, and high. Compared to the previous works that fails to generate high-quality outputs from high resolution inputs, this paper can work with high resolution images like those in the Challenge on Learned Image Compression (CLIC) dataset \cite{Toderici2020}.

\subsection{Adaptive Object and Background Compression and Decompression}
The objects detected in Figure~\ref{Fig3} are segmented from the original RGB image. Three of these objects are shown in Figure~\ref{Fig4}. 

\medskip
Motivated by the fact that correlated groups of pixels help to achieve a high compression ratio and reduce the bit rate, our approach compresses the segmented objects in addition to the background separately compared to feeding the RGB image to the compression model. As shown in Figure~\ref{Fig3} and Figure~\ref{Fig4}, the background image had more zeros due to the removal of the foreground objects. 

\medskip
It is much easier to encode a group of equal small values (zeros) for their pixels compared to encoding an object image which may have complex texture and high pixel values. Excluding the foreground regions, it is usual to have a background with close pixel values. For example, the street region in Figure~\ref{Fig3} has similar pixels to each other. Thus, it is easier to encode the groups of street pixels with a smaller bit rate. With respect to the foreground objects in Figure~\ref{Fig4}, it is obvious that such objects share close same information such as color. For example, the car in Figure~\ref{Fig4}a) has white pixels and thus these pixels together can be encoded with a low bit rate. The same case for the other objects in Figure~\ref{Fig4}.

\medskip
Based on this discussion, segmenting the objects from the background, and encoding each of them separately has a good effect in reducing the bit rate needed to represent each object in addition to the background itself. But considering only reducing the bit rate would only be helpful in the rate-distortion theory. To satisfy the rate-distortion-perception theory \cite{DBLP:conf/icml/BlauM19}, a GAN is used to compress the images to guarantee a high-quality output.

\medskip
Each object in addition to the background are applied to a GAN for compression the images with high quality \cite{mentzer2020highfidelity}. Powered by the GAN in \cite{mentzer2020highfidelity}, our work builds a high-fidelity GAN while using a very small bitrate. 

\medskip
According to the GAN architecture in Figure~\ref{Fig5}, the encoder E accepts the original image and returns a convolutional feature map with C channels. The quantized output is fed to the generator (decoder) G. The generator tries to build an image that is like the original image and follows its distribution in addition to following the extra information modeled by the conditional distribution. The outputs from the quantization and generator are fed to the discriminator D which forces the model to generate an output that is very close to the original image. Some noise drawn from a prior distribution may be added to the latent vector.

One of the advantages of this GAN is the ability to work with high-resolution images like the ones in the CLIC dataset. Given that the objects are segmented from the background, it makes it much easier to find more correlations between the pixels and produce a higher compression ratio while maintaining the quality.

\subsection{Image Reconstruction}
The final step in the pipeline used in this paper is to reconstruct the original image. Based on the binary masks and the outputs of the generator network from the GAN, the background is filled by the objects to get the output image.
